WHAT IS AQI?

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a standardized tool used to measure and report the quality of air in a specific location. It provides the public with crucial information about the level of air pollution and its potential impact on health. The AQI converts complex air pollution data into a single number, color, and category that is easy to understand. It is calculated based on the concentrations of key pollutants like PM2.5, PM10, nitrogen dioxide (NO‚ÇÇ), sulfur dioxide (SO‚ÇÇ), ozone (O‚ÇÉ), carbon monoxide (CO), ammonia (NH‚ÇÉ), and lead (Pb). Each pollutant is assigned a sub-index based on its concentration, and the highest sub-index determines the overall AQI. The AQI scale is divided into six categories ranging from "Good" (0‚Äì50) to "Severe" (401‚Äì500), with each range associated with a specific color and health advisory. High AQI levels can cause health issues, especially for sensitive groups like children, the elderly, and those with respiratory or cardiovascular conditions. Monitoring AQI helps individuals and governments take timely actions to reduce exposure and improve air quality through environmental policies and personal precautions.
